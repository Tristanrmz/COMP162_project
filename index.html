##Introduction #Dataset Link The dataset used in this project comes from the UCI Machine Learning Repository: https://archive.ics.uci.edu/ml/datasets/student+performance

##Dataset Description This dataset contains academic and demographic information about Portuguese secondary school students. Each observation represents one student. Variables include demographic information (age, sex, address), family background (Medu, Fedu), lifestyle behaviors (studytime, Walc, internet), and academic performance (G1, G2, G3).

For this project, the math (student-mat.csv) and Portuguese (student-por.csv) datasets were merged using shared identifying variables. After merging, the final dataset contains 382 students and 53 variables.

##Motivation -To understand factors associated with secondary school success.

-How can we increase or improve chances of success.

  • To see how academic performance can be influenced by many factors like study habits, alcohol consumption, parental education, and resources like the internet.

Interpretation:

The average final math grade for students without internet access is 9.41, while the average for students with internet access is 10.56. Although students with internet access have a higher mean grade, the p-value (0.0815) is greater than 0.05, meaning the difference is not statistically significant at the 5% level. Additionally, the 95% confidence interval includes 0, which further suggests that we do not have strong evidence of a true difference in final math grades between students with and without internet access in the larger population.

Conclusion:

This analysis examined several factors that may influence student math performance using the Student Performance dataset. While study time showed a small positive relationship with final math grade, the correlation and regression results were not statistically significant at the 5% level. This suggests that study time alone is not a strong predictor of final performance.

In contrast, past class failures showed a moderate and highly statistically significant negative relationship with final math grade. Students with more previous failures tended to earn substantially lower final grades, making prior academic performance one of the strongest predictors in this analysis. Other factors such as internet access and weekly alcohol consumption showed slight differences in performance, but these relationships were not statistically significant.
